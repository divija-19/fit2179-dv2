Act One

The Rise

From modest local gigs to a $3.4 billion industry — two decades of near-unbroken growth, a pandemic that wiped out the entire industry in a single year, and a record-breaking comeback that nobody predicted.

In 2024, Australians attended live concerts more than at any other point in recorded history — 31.4 million tickets and $3.4 billion in revenue. That surpasses the combined attendance at every AFL, NRL, A-League and cricket match played in Australia in the same year.

Act Two

The Map

Live music in Australia is geographically concentrated in two states. Where you live determines how much live music you can access — and that gap is widening every year.

New South Wales and Victoria together account for 64% of all live music revenue and 61% of attendance — while housing just 52% of Australia’s population. Queensland, with 20% of the population, captures only 16% of revenue.

Act Four

The Crisis

Record crowds. Record revenue. And yet — Australian music festivals are collapsing at an alarming rate. The paradox at the heart of the live music scene has never been starker.

In 2024 alone, Splendour in the Grass, Groovin the Moo, Spilt Milk and Lost Paradise were all cancelled. Bluesfest — a 35-year institution — announced its 2025 edition as its final. The stadium tours are booming. The grassroots festivals that built Australian music culture are dying.
